OptiQ   —   Options-Implied   Probability   Intelli- gence  What It Does  OptiQ takes live options market data and extracts the market’s implied prob- ability distribution for a stock’s future price.   It then overlays our DCF price target on that distribution to quantify exactly how much the market agrees or disagrees with our thesis — expressed as a probability.  In one sentence:   “The market gives our $620 target a 10.4% probability — if our fundamental work is right, that’s the mispricing.”  How It Works (6 Stages)  IB Market Data ￿￿> Calibrate 4 Models ￿￿> Blend (BMA) ￿￿> Extract PDF ￿￿> Plot + Report  Stage 1 — Data Collection  Pulls the full options chain from Interactive Brokers for the expiry closest to 30- 45 DTE. Filters out illiquid strikes (low bid, low volume, missing IV). Returns clean bid/ask/mid/IV data for all liquid calls and puts.  Stage 2 — Pricing Models  We use four models because no single model perfectly describes how markets behave.   Each one makes different assumptions about how stock prices move, and by combining them we get a more robust picture.  Black-Scholes-Merton   (BSM)   The   foundational   options   pricing   model (Nobel Prize, 1973).   Assumes the stock follows a geometric Brownian motion with   constant volatility   — the stock drifts upward on average and randomly fluctuates around that drift, with the size of fluctuations (volatility) staying the same over time. Produces a clean log-normal distribution of future prices.  Limitation:   Real markets don’t have constant volatility. The “volatility smile” in market data proves this — deep OTM options trade at higher implied vol than ATM options, which BSM can’t explain.   That’s why we need the other three models.  Single parameter:   sigma (annualised volatility).  Cox-Ross-Rubinstein (CRR)   A binomial tree model — instead of continu- ous math, it builds a discrete tree of possible price paths. At each time step the stock either goes up by factor   u   or down by factor   d , with probabilities chosen to match the expected return and volatility. We use 200 steps, which converges to 1

BSM but serves as an independent   sanity check   — if BSM and CRR disagree, something is wrong.  Why include it:   Validates BSM numerically. In calibration, CRR and BSM should produce nearly identical results. If they diverge significantly, it flags a data quality issue.  Single parameter:   sigma (same as BSM, but computed via a completely different method).  Merton Jump-Diffusion   Extends BSM by adding   sudden jumps   to the stock price. In BSM, the stock moves smoothly. In reality, stocks gap — earn- ings surprises, FDA decisions, geopolitical events cause the price to jump dis- continuously.   Merton models this as a Poisson process layered on top of the BSM diffusion: most of the time the stock moves smoothly, but occasionally it jumps by a random amount. The price formula is an infinite series of BSM prices, each weighted by the probability of exactly n jumps occurring in the time period. We truncate at 40 terms (converges well).  What it adds to the blend:   Captures fat tails and crash risk that BSM misses.   If the options market is pricing in an upcoming event (earnings, trial results), Merton will fit better than BSM and receive higher BMA weight.  Four parameters:   -   sigma   — diffusion volatility (smooth movement) -   lambda  — jump intensity (expected jumps per year, e.g. 0.5 = one jump every 2 years) -   mu_j   — mean log-jump size (negative = crashes more likely than spikes) -  sigma_j   — jump size volatility (how variable the jump sizes are)  Heston Stochastic Volatility   The most sophisticated model.   Instead of volatility being fixed (BSM) or jumps being added (Merton), Heston treats  volatility itself as a random process .   Volatility follows its own mean- reverting diffusion — it fluctuates randomly but is pulled back toward a long-run average over time. Crucially, volatility and the stock price are   correlated   (pa- rameter rho), which is why we see the volatility skew: when stocks drop, vol tends to spike (negative rho). Mathematically, Heston uses characteristic functions and numerical integration (Gauss-Laguerre quadrature with 64 nodes) rather than closed-form solutions. This is more computationally expensive but captures the vol smile naturally.  What it adds to the blend:   Explains the volatility smile without needing jumps. If the market is pricing a persistent vol regime (high uncertainty period, not just a single event), Heston will fit better than Merton.  Five parameters:   -   v0   — current instantaneous variance -   kappa   — speed of mean reversion (how fast vol returns to its long-run level) -   theta   — long-run 2

variance (where vol gravitates toward) -   xi   — vol-of-vol (how volatile the volatil- ity itself is) -   rho   — correlation between stock returns and volatility changes (typically -0.3 to -0.7)  Stage 3 — Calibration  For each model, we run   differential evolution   — a global optimization algo- rithm that searches the parameter space to minimize the total squared error between the model’s prices and observed market mid prices across all strikes simultaneously.   We use differential evolution rather than gradient descent be- cause the loss surface has local minima that gradient methods get trapped in. Each model returns: -   Best-fit parameters   (e.g., Heston’s calibrated rho tells us the market-implied stock-vol correlation) -   Residual SSE   — how well the model fits. This score is what determines the model’s weight.  Stage 4 — Bayesian Model Averaging (BMA)  Each model gets a weight inversely proportional to its calibration error — better fit = higher weight.   Weights are normalized to sum to 1.   For any strike, the BMA price is:  BMA_price(K) = w_bsm * BSM(K) + w_crr * CRR(K) + w_merton * Merton(K) + w_heston * Heston(K)  This is the key step that makes OptiQ more than just “we ran Black-Scholes.” BMA automatically adapts: if the market is pricing in jump risk, Merton gets upweighted.   If stochastic vol dominates, Heston leads.   If the market is well- behaved, BSM and CRR contribute equally.  Stage 5 — Breeden-Litzenberger Extraction  Takes the BMA call price curve C(K) across strikes and computes its second derivative:  q(K) = e^(rT) * d²C/dK²  This is the   risk-neutral probability density function   — the market’s im- plied probability distribution for where the stock will be at expiry. This result is model-free in theory (it’s a property of any arbitrage-free options market), but in practice we need smooth prices to differentiate, which the BMA provides. We fit a smoothing spline to the BMA prices first, then differentiate analytically. The resulting PDF is normalized to integrate to 1 and gives us: - Risk-neutral mean (market’s expected value of the stock at expiry) - Standard deviation, skewness, kurtosis - Percentiles and confidence intervals - Exact probability of reaching any price target 3

Correlation Matrix ( _correlation.png )  Two side-by-side heatmaps: •   Left:   Price Correlation   — how correlated are the four models’ out- put prices? CDF ( _cdf.png )  The cumulative distribution function — the “running total” of probability: •   Blue curve   — at any price K on the x-axis, the y-value gives P(stock < K at expiry). •   Red shaded region   — lower 25% of outcomes. •   Green shaded region   — upper 25% of outcomes. •   Yellow dots with labels   — key percentiles (P10, P25, P50, P75, P90) with their corresponding price levels. •   White dashed line   — spot price. •   Green line   — your target (if provided).
